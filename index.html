The Server Basin

A spatial analysis of where AI data center facilities are sited in relation to water stress and thermoelectric power infrastructure in the United States.

May 2026

What you're looking at

Data center facilities

The white circles are AI data center facilities across the United States. Click any facility to see what is publicly known about it: site name, operator, building footprint where available, and electrical capacity in megawatts where available. The dataset includes 1,380 facilities compiled from three independent sources and deduplicated. Most operators do not publicly disclose water consumption; only a small number of facilities have verifiable figures.

Baseline water stress

The yellow-to-red gradient shows baseline water stress by hydrological basin. The classification, developed by the World Resources Institute's Aqueduct framework, is calculated as the ratio of long-term annual water withdrawals to available supply, grouped into five categories from Low (under 10%) to Extremely High (above 80%). Aqueduct is also the framework used by major data center operators to assess water risk in their own siting decisions.

Thermoelectric power plants

The colored markers show the surrounding large-scale energy infrastructure: thermoelectric power plants over 100 megawatts (MW), sorted by fuel type. Megawatts measure a plant's electricity-generating capacity, so a 100 MW threshold filters out small or backup facilities and concentrates the view on plants that supply the grid at scale. Only thermoelectric plants (coal, natural gas, nuclear, petroleum) are included.

Findings

52.9%

Of US AI data center facilities are located in medium-high to extremely high water stress basins, compared to 43.7% of US basin area falling in those categories.

3×

Facilities are nearly three times more likely to be located in high water stress basins than a random distribution would predict (χ² = 333.03, p < 0.001).

+152.7%

Overrepresentation of facilities in high water stress basins specifically (withdrawal ratio 40-80%), where 314 facilities are observed against 124 expected under a random distribution.

+47%

Facilities in stressed basins have, on average, 47% more thermoelectric power plants within 50km than facilities in low-stress basins (t = 5.65, p < 0.001).

Sources and methodology

The map combines three datasets. Data center facilities were compiled by the author from three sources: commercial data center directories, the IM3 Open Source Data Center Atlas published by Pacific Northwest National Laboratory in 2026 (which draws on OpenStreetMap), and a set of 22 hyperscale AI training facilities compiled by Epoch AI in 2026. After deduplication using geographic proximity, name matching, and OpenStreetMap IDs, the merged dataset contains 1,380 facilities across the United States. The compilation is the author's own work and is not publicly redistributed (contact for access).

Water stress data is drawn from the World Resources Institute's Aqueduct Water Risk Atlas, version 4.0. Aqueduct divides the contiguous United States into 3,433 hydrological basins, each assigned a Baseline Water Stress score on a five-point scale from Low to Extremely High, based on the ratio of annual withdrawals to available supply over a 40-year period (1979 to 2019).

Power plant data comes from the US Energy Information Administration's Form EIA-860 (February 2025 update), filtered to thermoelectric plants (coal, natural gas, nuclear, petroleum) over 100 megawatts. Renewable sources such as solar and wind were excluded because they consume negligible cooling water. The filtered dataset contains 581 facilities.

A few notes on what this map does and does not show. The Aqueduct data captures long-term structural water stress, not year-specific drought conditions. The facility dataset is comprehensive but not exhaustive; many operators do not publicly disclose locations for commercial or security reasons. Water consumption figures are only available for a small number of facilities, drawn from corporate sustainability reports, environmental impact assessments, and water utility records. The map is a tool for visualizing patterns, not a substitute for facility-level audits.
